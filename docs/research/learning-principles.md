# Learning Principles and LMS Design Register

This register translates principles from *How Do We Learn?* and related research into product design commitments for the Learning Management System.

The register should be treated as a living design artifact. It is not a full literature review yet; it is the initial bridge between cognitive science, product requirements, and implementation issues.

## Current Principle Map

| Principle | Research basis | LMS design implication | Example product behavior |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Learning requires active meaning-making | The book emphasizes learning activities that make students use, analyze, compare, interpret, discuss, explain, and otherwise make meaning. | Do not treat lesson completion as proof of learning. Require active processing. | Learners explain a concept in their own words before seeing a model answer. |
| Retrieval practice improves durable learning | Roediger and Karpicke (2006) found that taking memory tests improved long-term retention. | Retrieval should be a core learning event, not just assessment. | Every module creates low-stakes recall prompts and cumulative checks. |
| Spacing beats massed practice for long-term retention | Cepeda et al. (2006) synthesized evidence for distributed practice across many experiments. | Important concepts should return over time, with scheduling tied to importance and performance. | A review queue resurfaces strategy concepts, formulas, and procedures over days or weeks. |
| Elaboration deepens understanding | The book connects elaboration with thinking about meaning; Dunlosky et al. (2013) rate elaborative interrogation and self-explanation as useful under the right conditions. | The LMS should prompt learners to connect new ideas to prior knowledge and explain why something is true. | Analyst learners explain why a factor matters in a manager due diligence case. |
| Working memory is limited | Sweller (1988) argues that excessive problem-solving load can interfere with schema acquisition. | Sequence learning from worked examples and focused tasks toward open-ended cases. | New analysts first inspect annotated examples before writing full memos. |
| Feedback should close the gap | Hattie and Timperley (2007) frame useful feedback around goals, current status, and next steps. | Feedback objects should include target, diagnosis, and action. | A rubric response identifies a weak assumption and assigns a follow-up retrieval/case prompt. |
| Transfer requires varied, realistic practice | Durable professional skill requires use across contexts, not one-shot recall. | Courses should include scenario variation, edge cases, and application tasks. | The same investment principle appears in manager review, portfolio construction, and risk discussions. |
| Metacognition can be wrong without calibration | The book highlights cognitive biases and the limits of intuition. | Track confidence separately from correctness and expose calibration gaps. | Learners rate confidence before answer reveal; analytics show overconfident weak areas. |
| Self-regulated learning matters | Zimmerman (2002) describes self-regulated learners as active participants in goal setting, strategy use, monitoring, and reflection. | Learners need goals, planning, progress visibility, reflection, and next-action guidance. | Personal dashboard shows current goal, overdue reviews, weak concepts, and reflection prompts. |
| Multimedia should respect cognitive constraints | Mayer's multimedia learning work emphasizes dual channels, limited capacity, and active processing. | Web app content should use diagrams and visuals only when they reduce load or improve understanding. | Strategy diagrams pair with prompts and examples instead of decorative media. |
| Evidence-informed teaching avoids neuromyths | The book and its reviews note attention to myths such as simplistic learning styles and left-brain/right-brain claims. | The platform should avoid unsupported personalization claims. | No learner is assigned a fixed "visual learner" path; adaptation is based on performance evidence. |
| Personal experience should generate hypotheses, not policy | Chapter 1 argues that intuition and experience are vulnerable to confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, cognitive dissonance, and bandwagon effects. | Treat trainer anecdotes, learner comments, and manager observations as qualitative evidence that needs triangulation. | A course author can attach field notes to a module, but claims remain marked as unvalidated until tied to outcomes or external evidence. |
| Evidence strength should be visible | Chapter 1 distinguishes scientific evidence from unsupported claims, pseudoscience, and popularity-driven beliefs. | Every principle, method, and recommendation should carry source type, claim status, confidence level, and review date. | A recommendation card shows "supported by replicated research," "promising internal pilot," or "unsupported/deprecated." |
| Causation requires stronger evidence than correlation | Chapter 1 distinguishes descriptive research from experimental research and warns against treating correlations as causal. | Analytics must separate association, prediction, and causal evidence. | A dashboard says "high completion correlates with assessment scores" rather than "completion caused learning gains" unless an experiment supports the claim. |
| Practical significance matters | Chapter 1 distinguishes statistical significance from effect size and practical importance. | Experiment reports should include sample size, statistical result, effect size, confidence, and operational relevance. | An A/B test may be marked statistically detectable but not worth deploying if the observed learning gain is too small. |
| Group-level evidence should not erase individual variation | Chapter 1 emphasizes that research effects usually appear across groups and do not guarantee outcomes for each learner. | Use research to set defaults, then adapt based on learner performance, role, and context. | A spaced review policy starts from evidence-based defaults but adjusts review cadence when an analyst repeatedly misses concepts. |
| Method labels are less important than active ingredients | Chapter 1 argues that methods such as project-based learning succeed or fail based on details, learner characteristics, goals, and context. | Model interventions by their learning mechanisms, not just method names. | A "case study" activity is tagged for retrieval, elaboration, feedback, and transfer rather than treated as effective because it is a case study. |
| Unsupported learning claims should be actively managed | Chapter 1 warns that pseudoscientific myths have opportunity costs and can harm learning. | Maintain a deprecated-claims register and prevent unsupported claims from driving personalization or course requirements. | The system blocks fixed "learning style" path assignment unless a future evidence review changes the claim status. |
| Memory is the basis of learning | Chapter 2 reframes memory as the faculty that enables all learning, not merely rote memorization. | Every learning objective should identify the type of memory or performance change it intends to build. | A module objective is tagged as semantic knowledge, procedural skill, episodic case experience, or metacognitive judgment. |
| Working memory is the conscious learning workspace | Chapter 2 describes working memory as the place where attended information is maintained, manipulated, and retrieved knowledge becomes usable. | Lessons should manage cognitive load and require active manipulation of information. | A complex analyst workflow is broken into annotated examples, short prompts, and progressive practice rather than presented as one long procedure. |
| Explicit learning requires attention and retrieval | Chapter 2 states that explicit memory depends on attention and is confirmed by retrieval back into working memory. | Passive exposure should not count as mastery; learners must recall, explain, select, or apply the knowledge. | Watching a strategy video unlocks a retrieval prompt and case application before completion. |
| Semantic and episodic memory serve different roles | Chapter 2 distinguishes context-rich event memory from meaning-based knowledge while noting their interdependence. | Case experiences should be followed by abstraction prompts that extract reusable concepts. | After a due diligence case, the learner identifies the general principle, warning signs, and transfer conditions. |
| Procedural skill requires practice and fluency | Chapter 2 treats procedural memory as implicit skill learning that develops slowly through practice. | Skill modules should use repeated practice, feedback, and fluency metrics rather than one-shot explanation. | Analysts repeatedly classify manager-risk scenarios until accuracy and response consistency meet a threshold. |
| Emotional conditioning affects learning context | Chapter 2 describes emotional conditioning as an implicit system that can trigger responses without conscious recollection. | Simulations should be challenging but psychologically safe, with debriefing and progressive difficulty. | A high-pressure investment committee simulation includes preparation, guided feedback, and recovery opportunities rather than surprise humiliation. |
| Memory is organized by meaning | Section 2.2 presents long-term memory as a network of semantically related knowledge, not an empty storage shelf. | Model learning objects as connected concepts, cases, procedures, prerequisites, misconceptions, and transfer contexts. | A risk-management concept links to prior analyst cases, required vocabulary, related procedures, common errors, and later portfolio-review uses. |
| Prior knowledge drives new learning | Section 2.2 uses schema theory and expertise studies to show that richer domain knowledge gives learners more hooks for new information. | Diagnose and adapt to what learners already know before assigning complex lessons or cases. | A new analyst takes a prerequisite check before starting an advanced strategy-selection module. |
| Prior knowledge must be activated | Section 2.2 distinguishes having relevant knowledge from bringing it to mind and using it to interpret new material. | Start modules with familiar scenarios, prediction prompts, or questions that mobilize relevant prior knowledge. | Before a lesson on operational due diligence, the LMS asks the learner to recall a past project where process controls mattered. |
| Active learning means learning by thinking | Section 2.2 argues that active learning is not mere doing; it requires thinking about the learning object in terms of meaning. | Activities should specify the target thinking action and make that thinking observable. | A case task asks learners to classify evidence quality and explain the classification, not merely assemble a presentation. |
| Understanding requires connected depth | Section 2.2 warns that rote school-context knowledge can become inert and hard to use elsewhere. | Prefer fewer concepts learned through multiple meaningful contexts over broad shallow coverage. | A core investment principle is revisited in manager selection, risk review, client explanation, and postmortem activities. |
| Constructivist learning still needs guidance | Section 2.2 separates constructivism as a learning theory from unguided discovery as a teaching method. | Use guided explanation, worked examples, questions, and structured practice to help learners construct knowledge. | Learners attempt a case after seeing an annotated model and answering prior-knowledge prompts. |
| Retrieval is a learning process | Section 2.3 distinguishes encoding, consolidation/storage, and retrieval, and argues that retrieval strengthens later learning. | Build retrieval into instruction, not only into final assessment. | A strategy lesson includes recall, explanation, and application prompts before the module is marked complete. |
| Retrieval demand should be explicit | Section 2.3 distinguishes familiarity, recognition, cued recall, and free recall. | Tag assessment items by the level and type of retrieval they require. | A quiz item is labeled as recognition, while a memo prompt is labeled as transfer/application retrieval. |
| Fluency is not mastery | Section 2.3 warns that rereading can create a feeling of knowing without later recall or use. | Separate exposure, recognition, recall, explanation, and transfer in learner analytics. | A learner who rereads a policy still receives a low-stakes recall prompt before the item counts as learned. |
| Desirable difficulty improves durable learning when calibrated | Section 2.3 connects effortful retrieval, spacing, and interleaving to desirable difficulties. | Schedule challenging but achievable retrieval, with hints and feedback when needed. | A review queue delays a concept until recall is effortful, then provides a hint if the learner stalls. |
| Feedback protects reconstructive retrieval | Section 2.3 notes that memory is reconstructive and can change during retrieval. | Pair retrieval practice with correction, model answers, rubrics, or expert review. | After a learner explains a due diligence warning sign, the LMS compares the answer to a model and flags missing conditions. |
| Spacing and interleaving beat massed practice for long-term use | Section 2.3 explains that massed practice can look better immediately while spaced and interleaved practice support durable learning. | Prefer distributed and mixed practice once learners have enough foundation. | Analyst cases rotate among manager selection, risk review, and operations issues instead of drilling one category in a block. |
| Memory is not a general muscle | Section 2.3 rejects the idea that arbitrary memorization improves general memory skill. | Teach transferable learning strategies rather than assigning unrelated memorization for its own sake. | The LMS explains retrieval, spacing, interleaving, and elaboration instead of requiring learners to memorize irrelevant lists. |
| Some learning requires conceptual reorganization | Section 2.4 argues that learners sometimes need to restructure prior knowledge rather than accumulate more facts. | Flag misconception-heavy objectives as conceptual-change targets with special lesson and feedback logic. | A valuation lesson is marked as conceptual-change work because common novice intuitions conflict with the target model. |
| Misconceptions are structured models, not just missing facts | Section 2.4 treats many errors as coherent prior explanations that resist replacement. | Diagnose likely underlying models and tailor remediation to the model, not only the symptom. | Two learners who miss the same question receive different follow-up prompts because their answer patterns imply different causal beliefs. |
| Explanation alone is insufficient when the old model survives underneath | Section 2.4 argues that learners can repeat correct language while preserving the original misconception. | Require learners to compare, predict, and reconcile models instead of only reading the correction. | After feedback, the learner explains why the initial answer seemed plausible and why the revised model better accounts for the case. |
| Conceptual change benefits from prediction failure and guided reconstruction | Section 2.4 emphasizes surfacing the old model, exposing its limits, and guiding the learner to a more useful alternative. | Build prediction tasks, discrepant cases, and bridging explanations into misconception-prone modules. | A workflow lesson asks the learner to predict an outcome, shows a counterexample, then walks through the revised causal model. |
| Transitional understanding should be tracked explicitly | Section 2.4 highlights hybrid or synthetic models as normal intermediate states. | Analytics should represent partial restructuring rather than only right/wrong states. | A learner is tagged as having a mixed model after using some correct terminology while still applying the old rule in new cases. |
| Transfer must be engineered, not assumed | Section 2.5 argues that learning does not automatically transfer from one context to another. | Treat transfer as an explicit product goal with defined future-use contexts and dedicated measures. | A module objective names the later analyst task where the concept must be used and schedules a delayed application case in that context. |
| Near and far transfer are different performance targets | Section 2.5 distinguishes similar-context transfer from distant-context transfer. | Separate routine application from novel application in assessments, analytics, and mastery rules. | The LMS records whether a learner succeeded on a familiar manager-review case or on a structurally similar but different operational-risk case. |
| Context variation and abstraction enable transfer | Section 2.5 argues that learners transfer better when they encounter the same principle across multiple contexts and explicitly extract the underlying rule. | Design case sequences that combine varied examples with comparison and abstraction prompts. | After three different due-diligence examples, the learner writes the common decision rule and the conditions where it no longer applies. |
| Deep understanding transfers better than rote procedure | Section 2.5 uses Judd and Wertheimer to show that conceptual understanding supports adaptation better than memorized procedure. | Assess explanation, boundary conditions, and adaptation, not just reproduction of the taught steps. | An analyst learner must explain why a valuation heuristic works and when it should be overridden in a different market setting. |
| Negative transfer should be anticipated | Section 2.5 notes that prior learning can interfere when an old pattern is applied inappropriately. | Track likely interference patterns and deliberately test exceptions and contrasts. | A training path injects a case where a previously reliable screening rule fails because the context changed. |
| Working memory is the bottleneck of conscious learning | Section 2.6 presents working memory as the limited workspace where attended information is held and manipulated. | Design lessons around what the learner must hold and process at once, not around raw content quantity. | A case view reveals only the current decision slice plus the few prior facts needed for that step. |
| Extraneous cognitive load is a product defect | Section 2.6 argues that irrelevant information and poor presentation consume scarce mental space without supporting learning. | Remove unnecessary text, clutter, redundant streams, and tangential examples from learning tasks. | The LMS suppresses decorative panels and collapses nonessential metadata while a learner completes a difficult task. |
| Intrinsic load should be regulated through sequencing and chunking | Section 2.6 argues that complex learning objects become manageable when new interacting elements are introduced progressively. | Break high-complexity lessons into steps, subskills, and staged integration tasks. | A new analyst learns a valuation workflow in sequenced checkpoints rather than one end-to-end simulation at the start. |
| Germane load should be protected, not eliminated | Section 2.6 treats meaningful processing that connects new knowledge to prior knowledge as desirable cognitive effort. | Reduce needless burden while preserving explanation, comparison, and meaning-making prompts. | After simplifying the interface, the LMS still asks the learner to explain why the chosen risk factor matters. |
| Multimodal presentation should use channels, not duplicate them | Section 2.6 draws on working-memory components and dual-coding ideas to distinguish helpful multimodal support from harmful redundancy. | Pair concise text or narration with visuals strategically and avoid reading dense slides verbatim. | A chart-based lesson uses a spoken explanation and short labels rather than a full paragraph mirrored on screen. |
| Overload can masquerade as inattention or disengagement | Section 2.6 notes that learners with reduced working-memory capacity may forget instructions, lose place, and abandon tasks. | Treat step-loss, abandonment, and instruction-forgetting as possible cognitive-load signals, not only motivation problems. | The system detects repeated restart behavior in a long workflow and offers a shorter guided version with checkpoints. |
| Domain knowledge reduces effective load within the domain | Section 2.6 argues that meaningful knowledge helps people cope better with working-memory limits in specific domains. | Revisit foundational concepts until they become available enough to support more complex cases and workflows. | Core investment vocabulary is overlearned through retrieval and examples before learners write full investment memos. |
| Deep knowledge is what enables expertise | Section 2.7 argues that experts differ from novices mainly through broad, meaningful, well-organized knowledge rather than rare innate talent alone. | Build expert performance as a long sequence of meaningful knowledge acquisition and practice, not as isolated "advanced skills" training. | An analyst track teaches core principles, patterns, and cases before expecting expert-like judgment in live scenarios. |
| Higher-order thinking depends on knowledge | Section 2.7 rejects the idea that knowledge and higher-order skills are competing priorities. | Treat reasoning, critical analysis, and creativity as downstream of meaningful knowledge rather than substitutes for it. | A strategy-analysis module requires strong conceptual prerequisites before learners attempt open-ended investment theses. |
| Practice must be deliberate, not just repetitive | Section 2.7 uses deliberate-practice research to distinguish purposeful skill improvement from repetition alone. | Practice tasks should have explicit targets, feedback, and progressive challenge. | A memo-writing sequence isolates thesis formation, evidence use, and risk articulation, with feedback on each before full memo production. |
| Complex skills should be decomposed and then reintegrated | Section 2.7 argues that many learning objects can be broken into components, learned in sequence, and later combined. | Authoring and curriculum design should support part-task progression followed by integrated performance tasks. | A due-diligence workflow is first taught as separate analysis steps, then reassembled into full committee simulations. |
| Expert blind spot must be designed around | Section 2.7 notes that experts often forget the subskills they now perform automatically. | Force expert authors and reviewers to externalize tacit steps, assumptions, and intermediate decisions. | The LMS requires a senior analyst to annotate hidden substeps in an "obvious" valuation process before publishing a lesson. |
| Automaticity should free cognition for harder work | Section 2.7 explains that practiced skills can become automatic and stop consuming working-memory space. | Identify which foundational skills should reach fluency so later tasks can focus on analysis, transfer, and judgment. | Basic screening calculations are drilled to fluency so later case reviews can concentrate on interpretation and decision quality. |
| Emotion claims should be mechanism-specific | Section 3.1 warns that discussions of emotion in education often mix up distinct phenomena such as motivation, emotional education, and emotional modulation of memory. | Tie emotionally oriented features to explicit mechanisms instead of broad claims about "learning through emotion." | A feature proposal must specify whether it aims to increase effort, support regulation, or alter memorability of an experience. |
| Emotion can support or disrupt learning depending on how it captures attention | Section 3.1 argues that emotions can make an experience more memorable, but can also divert attention away from the learning goal. | Design emotionally salient moments to support attention and meaning rather than trigger distraction or threat. | A high-stakes review screen uses calm framing and clear next steps instead of red warning-heavy language that hijacks attention. |
| Psychological safety protects cognitive performance | Section 3.1 notes that fear of mistakes or looking foolish can redirect attention toward self-protective thoughts. | Avoid unnecessary shame, threat, and public embarrassment in core learning flows. | Peer review is private by default and feedback is framed as diagnosis plus next action rather than public ranking. |
| Feelings are interpreted reports, not direct emotional readouts | Section 3.1 distinguishes automatic emotions from the later, culturally shaped interpretation called feelings. | Treat learner emotion check-ins as reflective self-reports with cultural and semantic flexibility. | A reflection prompt lets learners describe their state in their own terms instead of forcing one fixed emotion vocabulary. |
| Motivation should be modeled as goal-directed effort toward learning, not generic engagement | Section 3.2 defines motivation as a goal-directed state that sustains effort and argues that not all motivation is motivation to learn what matters. | Product engagement features should point toward the intended learning object and the thinking it requires. | A review queue rewards completion only when the learner attempts retrieval or application, not when they merely open the lesson. |
| Learning goals and performance goals produce different learning behaviors | Section 3.2 distinguishes learning goals from performance goals and notes that shallow assessments can reward the latter. | Use mastery-oriented framing and be cautious with status-heavy comparison features. | A curriculum dashboard emphasizes progress toward competencies and transfer tasks rather than public rank ordering. |
| Motivation depends on subjective value and expectations of success | Section 3.2 presents value and expectations as the main determinants of whether learners take action toward a learning goal. | Motivation support should act on relevance, usefulness, interest, and believable success paths. | Every module shows why it matters, what success looks like, and what the learner should do next. |
| Interest should be designed around the learning object, not decorative fun | Section 3.2 warns against confusing interesting with fun and argues that irrelevant entertaining details can compete with learning. | Engagement design should avoid seductive details and unrelated novelty. | A case module uses compelling real decision tradeoffs instead of badges, jokes, or decorative animations unrelated to the analysis. |
| Self-efficacy is domain-specific and instructionally actionable | Section 3.2 ties expectations to domain-specific self-efficacy rather than global self-esteem. | Measure and support confidence at the skill, task, and concept level. | After a learner misses a valuation task, the system offers a narrower prerequisite sequence and asks for a new confidence rating on that specific skill. |
| Early success and calibrated difficulty reinforce motivation | Section 3.2 argues that task difficulty should be calibrated and that visible progress strengthens expectations of success. | Build diagnostics, progressive sequencing, milestones, and low-risk practice into core flows. | A new analyst first completes a short guided memo component, gets feedback, and then graduates to a full memo after visible success. |
| Assessment alignment is part of motivation design | Section 3.2 notes that when assessments reward shallow preparation, students can succeed without durable understanding. | Align assessments with the actual depth of learning desired. | Transfer-oriented modules require explanation, comparison, and new-case application instead of only factual quizzes. |
| Beliefs mediate motivation through value and expectation | Section 3.3 argues that students' beliefs about learning, ability, and themselves shape the values and expectations that drive motivation. | Treat some motivation problems as belief-state problems that need different interventions than reminders or incentives. | A learner repeatedly avoiding quantitative modules is routed to a short diagnostic about perceived difficulty, prior experiences, and confidence, not just nudged to continue. |
| Outcome interpretation matters more than outcome alone | Section 3.3 argues that success and failure affect motivation through causal explanations, not raw scores by themselves. | Feedback should explicitly support productive attributions after both success and failure. | After a weak case response, the system explains which strategy broke down and offers a concrete retry path rather than implying lack of aptitude. |
| Controllability is a central feedback design variable | Section 3.3 emphasizes that causes seen as controllable and modifiable protect self-efficacy better than fixed or uncontrollable causes. | Product language should emphasize strategies, effort quality, revision, and supports within the learner's control. | Score reports pair misses with recommended study actions and practice modes, not with static ability descriptors. |
| Belief interventions work best when paired with effective strategies and real success | Section 3.3 reports that attributional and mindset-oriented interventions are strongest when combined with strategy instruction and success experiences. | Never separate motivational messaging from method support and progressive task design. | A module on difficult firm workflows combines a brief explanation about learnable skill growth with worked examples and milestone tasks that produce early wins. |
| Fixed learner labels are harmful even when positive | Section 3.3 warns against identity labels tied to ability and notes that flattering labels can also distort motivation. | Avoid static ability labels, talent badges, and identity-like learner categories. | The LMS does not call someone a “natural analyst”; it shows current demonstrated competencies and next areas to improve. |
| Mindset should be treated as domain-specific, mixed, and evidence-bounded | Section 3.3 presents fixed/growth mindset as context-dependent beliefs and later cautions that intervention effects may be modest or heterogeneous. | Model mindset-like beliefs at the skill level and test any intervention claims empirically. | The system may test belief-support prompts in difficult math-heavy training, but it does not assign users a global mindset score or promise broad effects. |
| Stereotypes can become self-fulfilling motivational constraints | Section 3.3 extends belief theory to gender, ethnic, and socioeconomic stereotypes that alter self-concept, interest, and expectations. | Recommendations, comparisons, and reporting must be reviewed for stereotype reinforcement risk. | Manager dashboards exclude identity-linked competence summaries and emphasize observed task performance plus support needs. |
| Social support changes motivation and performance | Section 3.4 argues that learners' perception of support from teachers and the surrounding environment changes how they respond to learning challenges. | Treat support as a stateful design variable in coaching, review, and collaboration flows. | A learner struggling in a difficult module gets contextual coaching and reassurance tied to next actions instead of generic reminders to continue. |
| Dialogue is the active ingredient of social learning | Section 3.4 emphasizes that social learning benefits come from discussion, explanation, comparison, and clarification rather than proximity or posting alone. | Social features should make learners explain, critique, and reconcile ideas. | A peer review task requires each learner to explain the partner's reasoning back in their own words before giving a verdict. |
| Scaffolding and competence asymmetry matter in collaboration | Section 3.4 uses Vygotsky's ZPD to argue that learning improves when guidance comes from adults or more competent peers. | Match mentors, peer helpers, and group roles based on what each learner can do alone versus with assistance. | New analysts are paired with more experienced reviewers for case walkthroughs that fade support over time. |
| Cooperative learning is not generic group work | Section 3.4 distinguishes true cooperative learning from common group assignments where one member does the work and others disengage. | Collaborative workflows need shared goals plus individual accountability for learning. | A team exercise gives one shared case file but requires each member to submit their own reasoning summary and transfer check. |
| Heterogeneous groups can improve learning when structured well | Section 3.4 argues against ability-only grouping and in favor of diverse groups under cooperative conditions. | Default peer grouping should consider productive diversity rather than only similarity. | A workshop groups learners with mixed strengths and requires peer explanation roles so stronger members teach and weaker members participate actively. |
| Expectancy and stereotype effects are real but usually bounded | Section 3.4 presents Pygmalion and stereotype-threat effects as meaningful but often small, context-sensitive, and methodologically debated. | Guard against evaluator bias and social threat, but do not overclaim massive causal effects without measurement. | Manager dashboards hide speculative potential scores and focus on observed work, recent progress, and concrete support actions. |
| Metacognition makes learning controllable | Section 4.1 defines metacognition as reflective awareness and control over one's learning processes, strategies, beliefs, and performance. | Build explicit planning, monitoring, and reflection into learning tasks. | A case module asks the learner to state the goal, choose a strategy, and explain why before starting the case. |
| Calibration requires evidence, not familiarity | Section 4.1 warns that learners often mistake familiarity for mastery and overestimate their understanding. | Pair confidence ratings with retrieval, self-testing, and performance evidence. | A learner who rates a concept as easy must answer a free-recall prompt before the item is marked stable. |
| Planning is part of expert performance | Section 4.1 contrasts expert planning with novice rush-to-procedure behavior. | Require plan-before-work checkpoints in complex tasks. | Before writing an investment memo, a learner submits a brief evidence plan and risk checklist. |
| Strategy choice is a teachable learning skill | Section 4.1 argues that effort is not enough when learners use weak strategies. | Recommend specific strategies tied to task type and explain the reason for each recommendation. | The system recommends retrieval plus spaced review for terminology, worked examples for a new procedure, and varied cases for transfer. |
| Self-regulation scaffolds should fade with demonstrated control | Section 4.1 describes autonomous learning as a result of explicit modeling, guided practice, feedback, and gradual withdrawal of support. | Fade metacognitive prompts based on behavior, not elapsed time. | A learner who reliably plans, monitors, and revises gets lighter prompts, while a learner skipping those steps receives stronger scaffolding. |
| Self-control supports learning goals | Section 4.2 defines self-control as the capacity to inhibit impulses, resist distraction, and choose longer-term learning goals over immediate rewards. | Treat attention, delay, and impulse management as designable learning supports. | A module protects a scheduled deep-work block and shows the next milestone that makes continued practice worthwhile. |
| Avoidable distraction spends limited control | Section 4.2 warns that inhibitory control is limited and that noisy or cluttered conditions can reduce attention and learning. | Remove unnecessary visual clutter, notifications, and decision friction from focused learning tasks. | A timed practice page hides nonessential navigation, chat badges, and recommendation panels until the session ends. |
| Autonomy works with structure | Section 4.2 links self-control development to environments that combine autonomy support with clear, consistent rules. | Offer meaningful choices inside explicit expectations, constraints, and feedback loops. | A learner chooses between two practice routes, but both routes share clear completion criteria and review checkpoints. |
| Regulation support should be targeted | Section 4.2 notes that self-control interventions matter most for learners with room to improve. | Trigger stronger self-control scaffolds from observed need rather than applying them uniformly. | A learner who repeatedly abandons long cases gets shorter milestones and attention prompts, while a learner with stable completion behavior gets fewer prompts. |
| Train self-control directly, not through worse learning conditions | Section 4.2 cautions against using distracting environments as a way to practice inhibition. | Teach regulation strategies through explicit routines, games, or structured practice while keeping core learning conditions focused. | A focus routine asks the learner to set a goal, remove distractions, and complete a short practice sprint instead of adding interruptions to the task. |
| Emotional state is part of learning readiness | Section 4.3 argues that strong emotions can capture attention, consume working memory, and shape decisions during learning. | Model emotional regulation supports as part of task readiness, especially before high-stakes work. | Before a difficult assessment, the LMS prompts the learner to name the concern, choose a reset routine, and identify the first controllable action. |
| Performance feedback is emotionally loaded | Section 4.3 explains that exams, grades, and feedback trigger performance-related emotions that affect future preparation and motivation. | Sequence feedback so it supports interpretation, next action, and perceived control. | A poor quiz result opens with the specific misconception, a short recovery path, and a retry plan instead of only a score. |
| Reappraisal is stronger than suppression | Section 4.3 presents cognitive reappraisal as a more effective regulation strategy than trying to suppress thoughts or emotional expression after the reaction peaks. | Help learners reinterpret difficulty, error, and threat before or during challenging tasks. | A case prompt reframes confusion as a signal to compare examples and request a hint rather than as evidence of inability. |
| Emotional regulation depends on clear support conditions | Section 4.3 argues that teachers can support emotional regulation through demanding but supportive environments with clear rules and expectations. | Keep challenge high while making criteria, support channels, and controllable causes visible. | A project workspace shows the rubric, help options, checkpoint dates, and examples of productive revision after failure. |
| Regulation routines should avoid counterproductive venting | Section 4.3 warns that thought suppression and cathartic venting can backfire, while breathing and reappraisal are more useful patterns. | Offer low-risk reset routines and avoid product features that amplify anger, shame, or public comparison. | A frustration prompt suggests breathing and reappraisal steps, not a public complaint wall or leaderboard comparison. |
| Resilience is productive recovery, not trait labeling | Section 4.4 treats resilience as recovery after adversity and warns against treating grit as a fixed explanation for success. | Track recovery behaviors and avoid permanent grit labels or scores. | After a failed case, the LMS records whether the learner reviewed feedback, changed strategy, and completed a revised attempt. |
| Persistence must include strategy change | Section 4.4 argues that trying again without analyzing errors or adjusting methods is unlikely to produce success. | Pair persistence prompts with metacognitive diagnosis and strategy revision. | A learner cannot simply click "try again"; they first identify the error type and choose a new practice strategy. |
| Long-term goals need near-term actions | Section 4.4 links grit with motivation and goal management, especially breaking distant goals into concrete actions. | Translate long-term learning aims into short tasks, milestones, and visible progress. | A semester goal becomes weekly retrieval sets, case drafts, feedback reviews, and checkpoint completions. |
| Growth beliefs need successful experience | Section 4.4 argues that belief work alone is insufficient; students must repeatedly experience effort and strategy leading to success. | Combine growth-oriented feedback with calibrated tasks and evidence of improvement. | A learner sees a revision history showing how changed strategy improved their score across attempts. |
| Productive struggle requires support | Section 4.4 warns that shielding learners from all frustration and leaving them unsupported in failure can both undermine resilience. | Design demanding tasks with scaffolds, feedback, and achievable next steps. | A difficult project includes checkpoints, hints, examples, and retry paths rather than either automatic rescue or sink-or-swim grading. |
| Teaching works through learner activity | Section 5 introduction argues that teaching advances learning by influencing what students do and think. | Design every instructional object around the learner action and cognitive process it should produce. | A concept lesson requires explanation, comparison, and retrieval rather than only presenting a reading page. |
| Feedback is a distinct learning process | Section 5 introduction separates instruction, feedback, and assessment as core teaching processes. | Model feedback as guidance for improvement, not merely a score or correctness flag. | A practice response returns the misconception, next step, and retry condition separately from the grade. |
| Assessment is evidence gathering | Section 5 introduction frames assessment as using performance evidence to infer learning. | Keep assessment data separate from engagement and completion data. | A learner who watches all videos is marked complete for exposure, but mastery is inferred only from performance tasks. |
| Method should fit the learning goal and learner state | Section 5.1 argues against a single best teaching method and treats effective instruction as choosing methods from a repertoire. | Select activity templates by objective, prior knowledge, motivation, and evidence rather than by method label. | A novice procedure lesson starts with modeling and worked examples, while an advanced transfer lesson starts with a case attempt before explanation. |
| Novices need explicit guidance for academic knowledge | Section 5.1 presents direct instruction as especially important for culturally accumulated, biologically secondary knowledge. | Default early pathways to clear explanation, examples, structured practice, and checks for understanding. | A first module on financial statement adjustments provides annotated examples before asking for independent case analysis. |
| Instructional scaffolds should fade with expertise | Section 5.1 applies the expertise-reversal effect to direct instruction and worked examples. | Reduce prompts, examples, and step-by-step guidance only when performance evidence shows readiness. | A learner who repeatedly completes guided valuation cases accurately receives partially completed examples, then open cases. |
| Sequencing protects working memory | Section 5.1 links effective instruction to small steps, component practice, and later integration. | Decompose complex tasks into coherent components while preserving a required reintegration path. | A memo-writing course separately practices thesis, evidence, risk, and recommendation sections before requiring a full memo. |
| Modeling should expose reasoning | Section 5.1 emphasizes modeling examples, procedures, expert thought, metacognitive strategies, and error handling. | Store exemplars with annotations that explain criteria, reasoning moves, and common errors. | A model investment memo highlights why each evidence claim supports or weakens the final recommendation. |
| Worked examples should transition to independent practice | Section 5.1 treats worked examples as novice supports that should be faded through completion prompts and self-explanation. | Use annotated examples, partial examples, and independent tasks as a staged workflow. | A learner studies a solved cash-flow forecast, completes missing steps in the next one, then builds a forecast from scratch. |
| Review should require retrieval | Section 5.1 frames review as active recall through quizzes, concept maps, explanations, or low-stakes tests. | Schedule review as retrieval tasks with feedback rather than passive re-reading reminders. | A weekly review asks learners to explain the difference between two strategy risks from memory before showing notes. |
| Questions should elicit explanation | Section 5.1 argues that useful teacher questions ask learners to explain, compare, justify, connect, and apply. | Tag prompts by the cognitive action they require and avoid treating silence after "any questions?" as understanding. | A concept check asks the learner to choose the better thesis and explain why, not just mark a definition as remembered. |
| Practice needs declared structure and feedback | Section 5.1 closes by tying effective practice design to feedback. | Practice records should specify target performance, support level, expected feedback, and transfer expectation. | A case-practice activity records whether it targets fluency, conceptual depth, independent transfer, or error diagnosis. |
| Feedback must include next-action guidance | Section 5.2 frames feedback around the goal, current performance, and what to do next. | Store feedback as goal, evidence, gap, and next action rather than a single comment string. | A rubric note identifies the missing risk evidence and links the learner to the next revision step. |
| Feedback level changes learning value | Section 5.2 distinguishes outcome, process, metacognitive, and learner-quality feedback. | Default product feedback toward process and metacognitive guidance, with outcome feedback used for correctness and trait feedback avoided. | A wrong answer response explains the reasoning rule to check next time instead of only saying incorrect. |
| Feedback timing should match the task | Section 5.2 argues that immediate feedback helps outcomes, while process and metacognitive feedback may benefit from delay. | Configure feedback timing by target and give learners room for attempt and self-correction. | A coding exercise shows test failure immediately but waits until after a revision attempt to show strategy feedback. |
| Feedback frequency should fade | Section 5.2 treats feedback as external metacognitive support that should decrease as learners gain control. | Adapt feedback density from heavy guidance to lighter checkpoints based on demonstrated competence. | A novice gets step-level hints, while an advanced learner gets summary feedback after completing the whole case. |
| Feedback interpretation is motivational | Section 5.2 shows that feedback can threaten identity, support growth beliefs, or change effort depending on learner interpretation. | Avoid trait labels and frame errors as controllable next actions. | A low score opens with the specific remediable gap and revision route rather than a learner status label. |
| Grades should not crowd out formative feedback | Section 5.2 warns that grades often capture attention and make comments feel like end-of-process judgment. | Separate formative comments from scores when possible and provide revision opportunities before certification. | A draft memo receives comments first, the learner revises, and the grade appears only after the second submission. |
| Assessment must align with the learning claim | Section 5.3 argues that assessment validity depends on whether the task measures the intended objective. | Require every assessment item to state its target objective, evidence type, and supported inference. | A reasoning objective cannot be certified by a vocabulary-only quiz. |
| Scores need measurement context | Section 5.3 distinguishes validity, reliability, accuracy, and precision as limits on interpreting grades. | Store score scope, uncertainty, rater reliability, and interpretation notes alongside assessment results. | A rubric score includes confidence and rater agreement rather than appearing as an exact measure of mastery. |
| Transfer requires transfer-oriented tasks | Section 5.3 warns that literal reproduction tests can miss whether learners can apply knowledge in new situations. | Use varied cases, scenarios, and open-resource tasks when the desired outcome is application or judgment. | An analyst training assessment asks the learner to apply a risk framework to a novel company, not recite the framework. |
| Formative assessment is evidence used for action | Section 5.3 defines formative assessment by how evidence is interpreted and used, not by the format of the test. | Connect assessment evidence to feedback, revision, reteaching, scheduling, or curriculum adjustment. | A quiz result automatically creates a targeted review task and changes the next practice set. |
| Assessment cycles serve different decisions | Section 5.3 distinguishes short-, medium-, and long-cycle formative assessment. | Model assessment cadence and downstream actions explicitly. | A same-lesson check changes hints now, a unit checkpoint changes next week's practice, and a quarterly review changes the curriculum plan. |
| Assessment can strengthen learning through retrieval | Section 5.3 closes by emphasizing the testing effect and retrieval-based learning. | Treat low-stakes assessments as learning events, not only measurement events. | A weekly cumulative quiz both estimates retention and strengthens later recall. |
| Neuromyth claims require evidence hygiene | The appendix warns that plausible brain-based explanations can become pseudoscientific educational claims when research is oversimplified or commercialized. | Require evidence status, mechanism, and review cadence before a brain-based claim can drive product behavior. | A "science-backed" recommendation remains blocked from personalization until linked to specific sources and outcomes. |
| Fixed learning-style labels should be avoided | The appendix rejects matching instruction to visual, auditory, or kinesthetic learner types while preserving the reality of learner differences. | Adapt to performance evidence, prior knowledge, accessibility needs, and task demands rather than style surveys. | The LMS never assigns a learner to a permanent "visual learner" path. |
| Modality should fit the learning object | The appendix distinguishes useful multiple representations from unsupported learning-style matching. | Choose text, diagrams, audio, demonstration, or examples according to what clarifies the target idea and reduces cognitive load. | A process lesson uses an annotated flow diagram because the process is spatial and sequential, not because the learner has a visual preference. |
| Plasticity should not become window panic | The appendix separates real sensitive periods and deprivation risks from exaggerated claims about narrow early-learning windows. | Avoid fatalistic age-window messaging and distinguish ordinary experience, deprivation risk, and lifelong learning. | Adult learners can still start a new domain; the pathway focuses on prior knowledge and practice evidence. |
| More brain activity is not the same as better learning | The appendix warns against equating more stimulation, more synapses, or more activation with greater intelligence or instructional quality. | Prefer educationally meaningful behavioral measures over vague brain-potential metrics. | A feature is evaluated by delayed recall and transfer performance, not by claims about activating more of the brain. |
| Hemispheric categories are not pedagogical categories | The appendix distinguishes real functional lateralization from unsupported left-brain/right-brain learner classifications. | Do not use hemisphere-type profiles, balancing exercises, or creativity claims as personalization logic. | Authoring tools flag "right-brain creativity pathway" language as an unsupported claim. |
| Knowledge graphs make prerequisites operational | *The Math Academy Way* argues that adaptive learning requires explicit topic, prerequisite, key-prerequisite, and encompassing relationships. | Represent learning objects as graph nodes and edges where the domain has real dependency structure. | A learner who fails an analyst-risk task receives practice on the specific prerequisite concept that caused the error. |
| Mastery estimates should carry evidence and confidence | Math Academy's diagnostic model infers learner knowledge from observed and graph-propagated evidence, while Ruiz Martin warns that assessment is an inference. | Store observed versus inferred mastery separately, with confidence, recency, and validity scope. | A diagnostic grants provisional credit for a prerequisite, then confirms or revises it during later practice. |
| Adaptive scheduling should combine retrieval, spacing, interleaving, and remediation | Math Academy operationalizes review scheduling through spaced repetition, interleaving, implicit review, and targeted remediation. | The scheduler should choose among new lessons, reviews, mixed practice, and remediation from learner state. | A review task is replaced by a more advanced case because the case also exercises the due prerequisite. |
| Implicit review should be tracked explicitly | Math Academy's FIRe model treats advanced tasks as partial reviews of simpler topics. | Record when later tasks exercise earlier knowledge, while preserving uncertainty around how much review credit was earned. | Completing a portfolio-construction case partially refreshes prior concepts in risk budgeting and liquidity. |
| Automaticity is a functional requirement for some skills | Math Academy emphasizes that low-level fluency frees working memory for higher-order reasoning. | Mark objectives that require fluent, low-prompt performance before dependent tasks. | A learner practices calculation or terminology until it no longer consumes attention needed for a judgment task. |
| Practice dose should be learner-specific | Math Academy argues that learners differ in how much practice they need, while Ruiz Martin cautions against fixed ability labels. | Adapt practice quantity and scaffolding to performance evidence without labeling the learner as a type. | One learner receives more component practice while another advances to mixed transfer cases. |
| Reference use can undermine retrieval | Math Academy's in-task coaching warns that constant reference use can prevent durable memory formation. | Track and coach reference use during retrieval-oriented tasks. | The system prompts the learner to attempt recall before opening a formula sheet, then asks for retrieval again after a targeted lookback. |
| Incentives need anti-gaming and motivation review | Math Academy treats XP and penalties as behavior-shaping tools; Ruiz Martin's motivation and feedback chapters warn about interpretation effects. | Use progress metrics only when they reward productive learning behavior and are monitored for shallow compliance or discouragement. | XP-like credit is paired with retention evidence and cannot be earned by rapid guessing. |
| Learner autonomy should be bounded by evidence | Math Academy limits task choice to protect sequencing, spacing, and assessment validity; Ruiz Martin preserves autonomy as motivationally important. | Offer meaningful choices among evidence-compatible tasks rather than unrestricted task selection. | A learner chooses between two practice contexts, but both satisfy the same due retrieval and prerequisite needs. |
| Product-effectiveness claims require separate review | Math Academy makes strong claims about acceleration, pass rates, diagnostics, and learning efficiency. | Store product claims as reviewable evidence objects before using them in requirements or external language. | A "4x faster" claim is marked needs-review until the comparison group, measure, and retention evidence are documented. |

## Design Questions to Preserve

- What does "mastery" mean for factual, conceptual, procedural, and judgment-based learning objects?
- How should the system balance spaced review with the natural cadence of work projects?
- Which analyst skills require human review rather than automated scoring?
- How should the system protect proprietary strategy content while still supporting rich examples?
- What data should managers see without undermining learner trust?
- How should personal learning notes and firm training records remain separate?

## Product Translation Rules

- A lesson is incomplete until the learner does something with the idea.
- A quiz is not only a grade; it is a learning event.
- A content item should carry its source, objective, assessment hooks, and review policy.
- A feature should not claim personalization unless it adapts based on evidence.
- A dashboard should recommend next actions, not merely display metrics.
- A professional training module should include realistic work products and rubrics.
- Internal strategy content should be modeled as sensitive by default.
- Memory type should drive assessment type: semantic knowledge needs recall/application, procedural skill needs repeated performance, and judgment needs varied cases with feedback.
- Every substantial activity should name the thinking it is designed to elicit; visible work is not enough unless it exposes the intended reasoning.
- Prerequisite checks and prior-knowledge activation are separate design moves: one verifies readiness, the other brings relevant knowledge into use.
- Retrieval should be designed as a recurring learning event with feedback, not as a one-time proof of learning.
- Scheduler logic should prefer spaced and interleaved retrieval over repeated identical practice in one sitting once a learner has succeeded.
- Transfer should be measured through performance in new contexts; completion inside the teaching context is not enough.
- Core concepts should be revisited across varied cases and followed by explicit abstraction of the underlying rule.
- Authoring defaults should aggressively minimize extraneous cognitive load while preserving the effort required for meaningful learning.
- External supports such as checklists, worked examples, note anchors, and step markers should be available during high-load tasks.
- Higher-order performance claims should be backed by evidence that learners have the meaningful knowledge base needed to support them.
- Decomposition should always be paired with later reintegration and authentic use so component practice does not become inert or demotivating.
- Emotion-related features should default toward clarity and safety unless a stronger emotional signal has a defensible learning mechanism.
- Motivation features should increase value and expectations for the learning object itself, not merely stimulate activity.
- "Interesting" should mean cognitively relevant, not ornamentally fun.
- Confidence should be measured and supported at the task and skill level, where it can actually guide sequencing and feedback.
- Assessment design is one of the strongest motivational signals in the system because it tells learners what success really means.
- Feedback should shape interpretations of performance, not just report performance.
- No learner-facing status system should harden into a trait label.
- Belief interventions should be tied to measurable success opportunities and strategic supports, not inspirational messaging alone.
- Evidence around mindset and belief change should be treated as useful but bounded, with explicit attention to subgroup effects and replication limits.
- Social features need a learning mechanism beyond “community.”
- Collaboration should optimize for explanation, scaffolding, and accountability, not just shared production.
- Supportive interventions should be sensitive to whether the learner experiences support as encouragement, pressure, or threat.
- Grouping and reporting choices should be reviewed for both cognitive effectiveness and socioemotional side effects.
